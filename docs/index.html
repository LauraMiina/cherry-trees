Cherry trees bloom earlier than before, and we shouldn't be happy about it

Cherry trees require a season of cold to blossom, which is why they succeed on the east coast of the United States. Climate change makes the trees bloom earlier, and in the long run threatens their whole existence.

Text and images by Laura Kukkonen

The last Saturday afternoon of March 2024 is busy at the Brooklyn Botanic Garden. Most trees are still bare, but the visitors are flocking to get pictures under the first flowering cherry trees. Pathways at the Japanese garden are filled with people gazing at the white and pink blossoms. The garden has many species of cherry trees, and most of them are still letting to wait for the blooms.

Cherry trees on the east coast of the United States first originated from Japan, which gifted some trees in the early 20th century. Most notable places to visit the cherry blossoms nowadays are Washington D.C. and New York. Cherry tree season marks the spring to be officially here. Warm and sunny days early in the spring are wonderful for picnic plans. But they also carry the message of climate change.

The flowering of the cherry trees has become a closely watched annual event.

Researchers have logged the peak bloom dates of cherry trees in Washington D.C. for a century.

What they have found is that during 100 years, the peak bloom date has moved to arrive five days earlier.

The same phenomenon has been observed also in Japan and New York.
